Personal Core Values


"Core values are the fundamental beliefs of a person or organization. These guiding principles dictate behavior and can help people understand the difference between right and wrong."

Based on the above definition I can assert that my personal core values are Honesty, Kindness and Respect.
The truth always matters to me more than anything else. If I value this quality so much is because when I was in Middle School I was the one who did not behave correctly: “Let him who is without sin cast the first stone”, right? Here’s what happened: I cheated during a Music test. I hadn't studied as well as I should have so I kept my book opened with all my notes under my desk and I shamelessly copied all the answers. Well, obviously I got caught by my teacher. I was not so smart as I thought, after all. He took both my test and my book without any admonition, I felt so ashamed of myself. And so what? You would say. The following day ,during an after school activity - choir practice - my Music teacher had the brilliant idea to return the book to me and tell everybody what I did. It was so humiliating and for a child like me, shy and insecure, it was a pretty bad beating.


“Your values create your internal compass that can navigate how you make decisions in your life. If you compromise your core values, you go nowhere.” ― Roy T. Bennett

Maybe this could seem a ridiculous example but I learnt a lesson: after that episode I have never cheated again, not once. Being honest has become an essential part of my being, along with kindness and respect: by growing up I realized that honesty cannot exist without them :“kindness without honesty is just lying, and honesty without kindness is just rudeness” (from the web).
I grew up in a family where honesty, trust and respect were the foundations of every relationship, foundations that had to be earned not given away. My parents have always encouraged me to speak openly, to confide in them and not hide anything. Of course listening to them was also part of the deal: I have learnt to let finishing other people's sentences , be open to other suggestions, embrace others’ ideas without judging from the start.

strengths and limitations


I took the Myers-Briggs personality test and got ISFJ-A -” The Defender”:


”The Defender personality type is quite unique, as many of their qualities defy the definition of their individual traits. Though sensitive, Defenders have excellent analytical abilities; though reserved, they have well-developed people skills and robust social relationships; and though they are generally a conservative type, Defenders are often receptive to change and new ideas. As with so many things, people with the Defender personality type are more than the sum of their parts, and it is the way they use these strengths that defines who they are.”


From the test result I identified the following strengths :
Reliable and Patient – Rather than offering sporadic, excited rushes that leave things half finished, Defenders are meticulous and careful, taking a steady approach and bending with the needs of the situation just enough to accomplish their end goals. Defenders not only ensure that things are done to the highest standard, but often go well beyond what is required.
Enthusiastic – When the goal is right, Defenders take all this support, reliability and imagination and apply it to something they believe will make a difference in people’s lives – whether fighting poverty with a global initiative or simply making a customer’s day.
Loyal and Hard-Working – Given a little time, this enthusiasm grows into loyalty – Defender personalities often form an emotional attachment to the ideas and organizations they’ve dedicated themselves to. Anything short of meeting their obligations with good, hard work fails their own expectations.

The test has also defined some of the weaknesses I empathize with:
Humble and Shy – The meek shall inherit the earth, but it’s a long road if they receive no recognition at all. This is possibly Defenders’ biggest challenge, as they are so concerned with others’ feelings that they refuse to make their thoughts known, or to take any duly earned credit for their contributions. Defenders’ standards for themselves are also so high that, knowing they could have done some minor aspect of a task better, they often downplay their successes entirely.
Take Things Too Personally –Defenders have trouble separating personal and impersonal situations – any situation is still an interaction between two people, after all – and any negativity from conflict or criticism can carry over from their professional to their personal lives, and back again.
Overload Themselves – Their strong senses of duty and perfectionism combine with this aversion to emotional conflict to create a situation where it is far too easy for Defenders to overload themselves – or to be overloaded by others – as they struggle silently to meet everyone’s expectations, especially their own.

During my first work experience I was working as a back-office employee for a company who provided insurance packages on behalf of a Bank to its clients. The final step before sending those packages away, consisted in printing the Welcome Letter for each customer and put both the letter and the assurance package in an envelope. The heading of each letter was selected based on the type of package chosen. My mistake was to print all the letters (something like over 200) with the same heading. I was so mortified for that ridiculous mistake that I soon as I noticed it, I immediately reported the issue to one of my superiors and I offered to skip my lunch in order to print the right letters. Armed with a lot of patience I spent that afternoon inputting all those letters in their respective envelope so they could be collected for shipping the same evening. From this experience I have learnt to keep more attention on what I am doing, check every detail before doing something and taking responsibility for my actions.

Since I was a child I always had a good role models to follow. Both my parents and all my teachers from preschool since high school have taught me to be kind, helpful and try to do my best in every situation. I consider consistency and hard-working two of the most important strengths both in my educational and career paths: I always make great effort to reach and deliver constant and reliable results. Remaining humble and grounded also help me dealing with every challenge: I don’t let accomplishments or rewards go to my head or affect my attitude, I am always polite to my colleagues or fellow student, my superiors or friends. One of the motto I have always tried to follow is “treat others how you want to be treated”.

Obviously, every coin has two sides and my weaknesses have accompanied me along with my strengths during my life. My commitment towards a task or a goal can be a double-edged sword: often I push myself too hard and I sacrifice my self-care until I find myself exhausted and stressed. My humbleness sometimes prevent me to advertise my achievements, so it could be easy for me to be underestimated in certain circumstances.


Strengths that might help me in my learning journey

I can definitely say that my enthusiasm, perseverance and diligence have a dominant role in my learning journey: the possibility to put myself to the test and learn new things are stimulus to keep me going, despite the obstacles that I may encounter, and reach my goal.

Resistance or tension between cowokers

Back in my country, I used to work as a receptionist in a textile industry. I had recently graduated from University and that was my first experience in that role so I had much to learn. Although at that time I wasn't sure it was the right job for me, I decided to try it anyway, on one hand because I wanted to earn some money and on the other hand because I needed to gain new skills for future opportunities. I used to receive a lot of phon-calls for a colleague that rarely was at her desk, so I had to write down all the messages from our client and pass them back to her. It often happened that the same client called three/four times each day without being contacted again so I had to invent excuses for her or I had to listen to all his complaints and be treated in a bad way. At that time I was too shy and insecure, maybe not even courageous enough to speak up, so I just kept doing my job silently, accumulating frustrations and stress. Until one day I could not bear it anymore, I started crying. I went to my boss and resigned from my position.
This happened eight years ago. Today I am a different person and I would have approached the problem in a different way. Probably I would ask my colleague for a meeting, explain my concerns, listen to her opinions and try to find a satisfactory solution together.
